Definition: Relation, Binary Relation, aRba \mathrel{R} baRb

Let AAA and BBB be sets. A relation between AAA and BBB, also known as a binary relation, is a subset of the Cartesian product A×BA \times BA×B. Given a relation R⊆A×BR \subseteq A \times BR⊆A×B and two elements a∈Aa \in Aa∈A and b∈Bb \in Bb∈B, we write aRba \mathrel{R} baRb to denote that (a,b)∈R(a, b) \in R(a,b)∈R.

Definition: Reflexive, Symmetric, Antisymmetric, Transitive

Let AAA be a set.

  • A binary relation R⊆A×AR \subseteq A \times AR⊆A×A is reflexive if aRaa \mathrel{R} aaRa for all a∈Aa \in Aa∈A.
  • A binary relation R⊆A×AR \subseteq A \times AR⊆A×A is symmetric if aRba \mathrel{R} baRb implies bRab \mathrel{R} abRa for all a,b∈Aa, b \in Aa,b∈A.
  • A binary relation R⊆A×AR \subseteq A \times AR⊆A×A is antisymmetric if aRba \mathrel{R} baRb and bRab \mathrel{R} abRa implies a=ba = ba=b for all a,b∈Aa, b \in Aa,b∈A.
  • A binary relation R⊆A×AR \subseteq A \times AR⊆A×A is transitive if aRba \mathrel{R} baRb and bRcb \mathrel{R} cbRc implies aRca \mathrel{R} caRc for all a,b,c∈Aa, b, c \in Aa,b,c∈A.

Definition: Equivalence Relation

An equivalence relation on a set AAA is a binary relation R⊆A×AR \subseteq A \times AR⊆A×A that is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.

Definition: Converse, Inverse

Let AAA and BBB be sets. The converse or inverse of a relation R⊆A×BR \subseteq A \times BR⊆A×B is the relation R−1⊆B×AR^{-1} \subseteq B \times AR−1⊆B×A defined by

Definition: Function, Map, Domain, Codomain, f:A→Bf: A \to Bf:A→B, f(a)f(a)f(a), Maps

Definition: Injective, Injection, f:A↪Bf: A \injto Bf:A↪B

Let AAA and BBB be sets. A function f:A→Bf: A \to Bf:A→B is injective if a1≠a2a_1 \ne a_2a1​=a2​ implies f(a1)≠f(a2)f(a_1) \ne f(a_2)f(a1​)=f(a2​) for all a1,a2∈Aa_1, a_2 \in Aa1​,a2​∈A. Let AAA and BBB be sets. A function f:A→Bf: A \to Bf:A→B is bijective if it is both injective and surjective. We call a bijective function a bijection, and we write f:A↪ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣ ⁣→Bf: A \bijto Bf:A↪→B to denote that fff is a bijective function from AAA to BBB.

Only Bijections have Inverses

Theorem: Let AAA and BBB be sets, and let f:A→Bf: A \to Bf:A→B be a function. The converse relation f−1f^{-1}f−1 is a function if and only if fff is a bijection.

Proof: Suppose f−1f^{-1}f−1 is a function. Conversely, suppose fff is a bijection. For every b∈Bb \in Bb∈B, the surjectivity of fff implies that there exists an a∈Aa \in Aa∈A such that afba \mathrel{f} bafb, and the injectivity of fff implies that this aaa is unique. Hence, f−1f^{-1}f−1 is a function. ∎

Definition: Hausdorff Space

A Hausdorff space is a topological space (X,T)(X, T)(X,T) that has the following property: for all x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X, if x≠yx \ne yx=y, then there exist U,V∈TU, V \in TU,V∈T such that x∈Ux \in Ux∈U, y∈Vy \in Vy∈V, and U∩V=∅U \cap V = \varnothingU∩V=∅.

Definition: Basis

Let (X,T)(X, T)(X,T) be a topological space. A basis of (X,T)(X, T)(X,T) is a collection of open sets B⊆TB \subseteq TB⊆T that has the following property: for every open set U∈TU \in TU∈T, there exists a sub-collection C⊆BC \subseteq BC⊆B such that U=⋃CU = \bigcup CU=⋃C.

In other words, a basis is a collection of open sets that allows every open set to be written as a union of basis elements.

Definition: Second-Countable

A topological space is second-countable if it admits a countable basis.

Definition: Manifold, Dimension

A manifold of dimension n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N, also called an n\boldsymbol{n}n-manifold, is a second-countable locally Euclidean Hausdorff space.

If manifolds were not required to be Hausdorff, then the line with two origins would be a one-dimensional manifold. Similarly, if manifolds were not required to be second-countable, then the long line would be a one-dimensional manifold.

Let n∈Nn \in \Nn∈N. The factorial of nnn, denoted by n!n!n!, is the product of the numbers {1,2,…,n}\{1, 2, \dots, n\}{1,2,…,n}. In other words,

n!≔1×2×⋯×n. n! \coloneqq 1 \times 2 \times \dots \times n. n!:=1×2×⋯×n.

We define 0!≔10! \coloneqq 10!:=1 in accordance with the convention that the product of the empty set ∅\varnothing∅ is the multiplicative identity 111.
